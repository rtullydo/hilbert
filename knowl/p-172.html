At issue is how we define the integral. The Riemann integral is the familiar \(\int f \, \dd x\) that we learn in elementary calculus. Many more functions \(f\) on \([0,1]\) will satisfy

\begin{equation*} \int_0^1 \abs{f}^2 \dd t \lt \infty, \end{equation*}

than \(C[0,1]\text{,}\) and yet even this space is incomplete. We need a better integral. The Lesbesgue integral is significantly more challenging to develop than the Riemann integral, and we will not attempt that in this course. It is enough for us to accept that a more powerful notion of integration exists so that the space of square-integrable functions is complete with respect to (2.1.1).
